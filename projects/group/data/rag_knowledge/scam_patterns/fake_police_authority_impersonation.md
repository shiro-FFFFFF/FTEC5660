# Scam Pattern: Fake Police / Authority Impersonation

## Summary
This scam involves an attacker pretending to be police, law enforcement, government, or cybercrime investigators. The victim is pressured into believing they are involved in a criminal case or financial investigation.

## Typical Channels
- phone call
- SMS follow-up
- chat follow-up

## Typical Narrative
- caller claims to be police or investigator
- says victim's account is linked to crime, money laundering, or cybercrime
- creates fear and urgency
- instructs victim not to tell others
- may direct victim to move money to a so-called safe account
- transfer attempt usually follows after fear and secrecy are established

## Common Signals
- words like police, arrest, investigation, cybercrime
- secrecy instructions such as do not tell anyone
- urgent demand for immediate action
- request to transfer funds for verification or protection
- authority-based pressure

## Common Social Engineering Tactics
- authority impersonation
- fear / threat
- urgency
- isolation
- payment redirection

## Why It Is Risky
A transfer in this context is high risk because the transfer is not being initiated independently by the user. It is being induced through fear, authority, and time pressure.

## Transfer Risk Cues
- new recipient
- large amount
- transfer shortly after suspicious call
- recipient presented as safe account, holding account, investigation account
- bank account and recipient name may not align with claimed authority

## Recommended User Action
- do not transfer money
- end the call
- contact police or bank through official channels
- verify independently
- do not keep the matter secret from trusted family or bank staff

## Recommended Analyst Action
- flag as authority impersonation pattern
- preserve phone number and transcript
- escalate if a transfer is attempted after a recent call
